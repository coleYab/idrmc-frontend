Chapter One
Introduction
Overview
Integrated Information Systems for Disaster Response and Emergency Management - hereafter referred to as ISDREM - is an all-inclusive digital platform that aims to digitalize Ethiopia's disaster management system. It coordinates communication, improves coordination and real-time situational awareness among government agencies, first responders and humanitarian organizations. In contrast to traditional paper-based workflow, ISDREM proposes a data-driven approach to efficient reporting, speedy information sharing and resources distribution optimization. Its system of centralized solutions which allows decision-makers to have real-time information on disasters, which can be easily and promptly taken (United Nations Office for Disaster Risk Reduction [UNDRR], 2024a; Ethiopian Disaster Risk Management Commission [EDRMC], 2023).
Information systems that are integrated around the world have contributed a lot in terms of disaster preparedness and response. Such nations as Japan, India and Kenya have used real-time analytics and geospatial monitoring to minimize response time and economic damages (World Bank Group, 2023a; International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies [IFRC], 2024b). Based on these developments, ISDREM is aligned to the Digital Transformation Strategy 2025 of Ethiopia, which focuses on using ICT as a national resilience measure and sustainable development (Ministry of Innovation and Technology, 2025).
Background of the Organization
The coordinated governmental institution of disaster prevention, preparedness, recovery and response related to disasters in Ethiopia is the Ethiopian Disaster Risk Management Commission (EDRMC). Having been formed in accordance with the National Disaster Risk Management Policy of 2013 and being under the Ministry of Peace, it is the main organization that helps to protect the country in case of natural or manmade disasters (IFRC, 2013).
The Commission’s core functions include:
Policy and Strategy: Formulating, revising, and monitoring national disaster management policies and implementation strategies.
Operational Readiness: Maintaining stand-by capacities for timely responses to national disasters and issuing early warnings in cooperation with domestic and international partners.
Resource Management: Directing the National Disaster Prevention and Preparedness Fund and the National Food Security Reserve to assist food-insecure households.
Coordination and Capacity Building: Harmonizing emergency aid programs, overseeing NGO activities, and providing institutional support to regional and local offices.
Mainstream: Integrating disaster risk management into sectoral policies across various ministries and institutions.
EDRMC as a legal entity has the power to own property, enter a contract, raise funds both on the national and international front to fill capacity gaps. Finally, Ethiopia has a disaster risk management system that is supported by the Commission to maintain resilience and protect the communities against repeated dangers (IFRC, 2013).
Statement of the Problem
The present-day disaster response system of Ethiopia has serious shortcomings in terms of both operations and information that are crippling in the effective and prompt management of the crisis. Although the institution has institutional frameworks, such as the DRMC, the lack of a unified digital platform leads to the following significant issues:
Delayed Incident Detection and Reporting: Field agents and citizens rely on phone calls, paper forms, or physical travel to report disasters, leading to response delays of several hours to days, especially in rural and remote areas (UNDRR, 2024a).
Poor Inter-Agency Coordination: Government bodies, NGOs, and first responders operate in silos with no shared real-time platform, resulting in duplicated efforts, misallocated resources, and communication breakdowns during crises (Beco & Sisay, 2024; EDRMC, 2021).
Inefficient Resource Management: There is no centralized system to track the availability, location, or deployment status of critical supplies (e.g., food, medicine, tents, vehicles), leading to wastage, shortages in affected areas, and slow mobilization (EDRMC, 2023b).
Limited Public Awareness and Early Warning Reach: Early warnings issued by the NMA or DRMC are disseminated via radio or TV, which exclude non-listeners, illiterate populations, and offline communities. SMS alerts are inconsistent and not geo-targeted (UNDP, 2025a; United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs [OCHA], 2024a).
Lack of Data-Driven Decision Making: Decision-makers lack access to real-time analytics, GIS-based risk maps, or predictive insights, forcing reactive rather than proactive planning (UNDRR, 2024a).
Manual Processes and Error-Prone Data Flow: Paper-based reporting introduces transcription errors, data loss during transport, and inability to verify authenticity or timeliness of information (Beco & Sisay, 2024).
To conclude, the existing disaster risk management system in Ethiopia is strongly limited by the disjointed communication channels, manual data processing and the absence of real time coordination. It is these systemic inefficiencies, whether it is a slow incident reporting post or the sub-optimal distribution of life-saving resources, that create a very big gap between the disaster occurring and an effective response being provided. The EDRMC is still struggling to transition to a data-driven, proactive disaster management framework, rather than a reactive relief model, without a centralized, digital ecosystem consisting of risk mapping based on GIS, automated resource tracking, and Geo-targeted early warning systems. The need to address these technological gaps is not just an operational upgrade but a key requirement to the improvement of national resilience and minimizing the loss of lives.

Feasibility Study
A feasibility study is a broad study that determines the viability and practicality of a given project by analyzing some of the vital issues like technical, economic, operational and legal ones. This is necessary as it assists in identifying possible risks, constraints and opportunities at the initial stage which permits resources to be used effectively and align with the organization's objectives.
In the case of the ISDREM it evaluates the capacity of the system to improve the disaster response of the EDRMC.
Technical feasibility
The ISDREM project has very decent technical capabilities based on an up-to-date and solid technology stack. It uses React and React Native which makes the system applicable on a wide range of devices and works in low connectivity conditions. This will enable real-time update provision.
The back-end using Node.js can be a highly performative and scalable environment with the capability to handle large amounts of incoming real-time data. And, by using PostgreSQL with PostGIS extensions, it would support the complex geospatial queries and mapping demands required for Geo-tagging and resource allocation.
These selected technologies are open-source, well documented and have huge communities of developers which significantly reduce risks of development and guarantee the ability to maintain and scale in the future. In addition, we all as a group have experience of working with these technologies, adding to the reason why the project will be technically sound.
Economic feasibility
The ISDREM project demonstrates high economic feasibility as it is based on open-source frameworks and does not require the use of expensive licensing and proprietary software payments. The development will be based on the utilization of what the EDRMC already has in place in terms of communication and computing infrastructure to minimize the start-up cost.
The long-term return on investment (ROI) is significant and measurable. ISDREM is projected to decrease the financial cost of disaster response.
These benefits can be categorized into tangible and intangible benefits.
Tangible Benefits:
Cost Savings in Disaster Response:
Reduced Economic Losses: The incident reporting, alerts and GIS implementation of the system allows making decisions quickly and informed and can reduce the damage to up to 30 percent through prompt warnings (World Meteorological Organization [WMO], n.d.). In Ethiopia, the country loses 1-1.5 percent of GDP every year because of climatic reasons (World Bank Group, 2024).
Formula for estimating:
Savings= (Total At-Risk Value) * (Probability of Event) * 0.3
If a region has 30 million ETB in movable assets and a flood occurs, saving 30%
Time Savings: It improves situation awareness and decision making by streamlining and automating the coordination process by at least just 1 hour. Manual coordination formerly led to “resource-paralysis” during the first 48 hours of an event which reduce the gap of information and action by 40-60 percent (EDRMC, 2023b). For instance, if 1,000 staff hours are saved annually across 10 regions at an average professional rate of 500 ETB/hr.
Operational Cost Reduction: The cost of fuel can be reduced by using GIS routing reduces inefficient travel (World Food Programme [WFP], 2025). For instance, if hundreds of million ETB is annually spent on trucking food and non-food items. We can gain 2% efficiency if there is GIS for routing specially in Afar or Somali region. 
Optimization of Resource and Cost: The definition of resource and inventory management and donation tracking prevent duplication and enhances aid efficiency (World Bank, 2022).

Operational Feasibility
The ISDREM system is operationally feasible because it is highly relevant and can be easily integrated into the existing working environment. The system is designed with simple and user-friendly interfaces that match the daily work of EDRMC officials, field agents, and first respondents. Because of this, only minimal training is required, and users can learn the system in a short period of time.
The system also supports mobile incident reporting and automated resource tracking, which directly addresses the main challenges identified in the Statement of the Problem. These features provide immediate and practical value to users during real disaster response activities. Since the system aligns well with the existing disaster management cycle, it is expected to have a high level of user acceptance and can be smoothly incorporated into the standard operating procedures of EDRMC. This improves response capacity without requiring major changes to the current organizational structure.
Legal Feasibility
Implementing the ISDREM system in Ethiopia is legally feasible, aligning with the Ethiopian Disaster Risk Management Proclamation No. 1386/2025 and the National Policy and Strategy on Disaster Risk Management, which provide a comprehensive framework for disaster risk reduction, response and management. The system supports key principles such as early warning, official disaster declaration and multi-hazard impact-based approaches, as outlined in these policies. Additionally, data handling complies with the Personal Data Protection Proclamation No. 1321/2024, ensuring protection of sensitive personal information collected during incidents, with provisions for data subject rights and secure processing by controllers like the EDRMC. This alignment promotes transparency, accountability and adherence to international standards like the Sendai Framework, while mitigating risks of non-compliance through built-in audit trails and privacy-by-design features (EDRMC, 2025b).
Significance of the project
The importance of the ISDREM project is multidimensional, as it influences the results of humanitarian efforts, economic stability and long-term governance. These are:
Lifesaving impact: It enables the spread of early warnings among the most vulnerable groups faster and reduce the response time of first respondents drastically through the improved coordination of the resources.
Economic Protection: The platform minimizes property and critical infrastructure loss by mobilizing resources in time and implementing specific protection, which enhances the resilience of the whole nation.
Data-driven governance and policy making: The centralized and standardized statistics on ISDREM will bring the EDRMC to data-driven policy, where the information is abundant and reliable to be analyzed to implement the disaster risk reduction strategies in the long-term, policy formulation and risk aversion to future crises.
Beneficiaries of the project
The ISDREM project is aimed at providing significant advantages to various stakeholders engaged in the processes of disaster response and emergency management both directly and indirectly. The system seeks to transform the historic disaster management framework to provide an interdependent setting with live data integration, geospatial applications and mobile availability. This brings in efficiency, transparency and speed of response, which will greatly influence the following beneficiaries:
EDRMC Staff and High-Level Decision-Makers: The primary beneficiaries of the ISDREM system are the Ethiopian Disaster Risk Management Commission (EDRMC) staff and high-level decision-makers. Historically, the disaster management process is characterized by disjointed sources of data, manual reporting and slow insights, which may impede the ability to create effective coordination and distribution of resources. These users can use ISDREM to have a single command platform in real time that includes analytics, risk maps based on GIS to allow them to make data-driven decisions that can reduce losses and maximize responses (Beco & Sisay, 2024; EDRMC, 2021). Also, operations are made transparent by the system, which will minimize risks of inefficiencies or misunderstandings in times of crisis.
First Responders (Police, Firefighters, Medical Teams): The system will also be beneficial to the First responders in the form of police, firefighters and medical teams. This is made possible by ISDREM, which provides easy access to real-time incident reporting, resource tracking and geo-tagged updates which enable these operational teams to mobilize easily and coordinate on-site activities without silos. This puts rapid interventions on equal terms, especially in distant locations, with mobile applications of online and SMS of offline reporting and instant notifications, thus saving lives and shortening response times (IFRC, 2024a; UNDRR, 2024a).
Non-Governmental Organizations (NGOs such as the Red Cross and Aid Organizations): NGOs and aid organizations engaging in disaster response will also enjoy the benefits of the central hub of ISDREM in inter-agency coordination. The system also creates the possibilities of an improved picture of resource availability and demands to allow these groups to evade overlapping work and provide proper distribution of aid (IFRC, 2024b; OCHA, 2024a).
Local Communities and Citizens: Local communities and general users will directly benefit by receiving timely early warning alerts, public awareness features and reporting of an incident through an easy mobile interface. ISDREM increases resilience through sharing of geo-targeted messages and facilitating the use of citizen-sourced data, which will increase the general preparedness to disasters and mitigate their vulnerability. The transparency of the system reduces the chances of marginalization of rural or illiterate communities, which is compliant with the national standards of all-inclusive emergency management (EDRMC, 2025a; UNDP, 2024a).
Methodologies
A software development methodology provides a well-organized way of developing a project in different spheres and organizations based on the choice on which model will correspond to their needs. It is a logical sequence of phases such as requirements analysis, design, implementation, testing, deployment and maintenance. This provides an effective way of managing and controlling quality (Alazzawi, 2023).
For the development of the ISDREM system, a clear methodology is required to ensure that the system is reliable, user-centered, and suitable for disaster management operations in Ethiopia. The methodology used in this project consists of two main parts, requirements gathering and system development methodology. Together, these approaches ensure that ISDREM is built based on real user needs and is developed in an efficient and flexible manner.
Data Collection Methods
Requirements gathering is the process of identifying and understanding the needs, expectations, and constraints of system users and stakeholders. In ISDREM, this step is critical because disaster management systems must align closely with real operational workflows. The requirements gathering process uses interviews and document analysis to collect accurate and relevant information (Kotonya & Sommerville, 2017).
The data collection process for developing ISDREM utilizes interviews and document analysis to ensure that the system effectively meets the needs of disaster management practitioners.
Interviews
Conducting interviews with stakeholders enables us to get rich qualitative data on the actual issues, processes and needs of the system.
It helps in identifying the functional requirements, usability needs and gaps of the current process and system.
The interviewees are the IT staff members, Early warning staff members, Post Disaster Assessment staff members and top-level managers.
The interview will be based on the existing gaps in the existing system in the incident reporting, alert sharing and resource tracking and geospatial mapping.
As a result, this information will help identify the functional requirements and user roles.
Document Analysis
It will provide evidence-based insights on established standards and effective elements from national and international systems
By analyzing key documents on the early warning systems, coordination and geospatial tools we identify the common standards for alerts, resource management, incident handling and data sharing.
The data gained from the document analysis helps in feature selection and compliance with standards.

Problems of the Current System (Using the PIECES Framework)
The existing disaster management and emergency response systems at Ethiopian Disaster Risk Management Commission (EDRMC) is based on integrating fragmented digital solutions to include Livelihoods, Early Assessment and Protection (LEAP) system, which is a vulnerability assessment system, DesInventar system, which is a historical disaster databases system and the National Incident Management System (NMIS), which is a basic coordination protocol system and mostly manual and paper-based. These tools, although effective in individual functions such as early warning prediction or logging of events, work in silos, which are not interoperable causing great inefficiency in real-time operations (UNDRR, n.d.).
To systematically discuss these failures, we used the PIECES framework (Performance, Information, Economy, Control, Efficiency and Service). It is a methodical diagnostic instrument for assessing information systems. This framework identifies essential gaps that prioritize problems with six categories with each issue being quantified where applicable based on EDRMC report data, international standards (UNDRR, 2024a) and Ethiopian case studies (2023 floods and 2024 drought responses). It is observed, through the analysis, that there are obvious discrepancies between the prevailing scattered situation and the intended integrated, real-time, which is why ISDREM must fill in these gaps.
Performance Problems
Slow data processing and unreliable accessibility of the current system is a setback to its performance especially in remote or low connectivity locations where most disasters take place in Ethiopia. Field reporting by people through paper forms or phone calls leads to a response time delay of 24-72 hours as in the case of the 2023 Afar flooding when preliminary reports to headquarters took an average of 48 hours (OCHA, 2025). Online platforms such as DesInventar can deliver retrospective data with no real-time querying functions, leading to system delays whenever there are peak events - search times of over 10 minutes to make simple searches.
Gap: The required incident logging and data retrieval time is less than 1 hour; the delays at hand increase 20-30% casualty rates during fast onset disasters (World Bank Group, 2023b).
Information Problems
The lack of a central repository has a devastating effect on the accessibility and accuracy of information exchange because of the lack of complete or up-to-date data dissemination. As an example, LEAP creates vulnerability maps without combining with NIMS to update in real-time, which creates a situation where 40 percent of field reports are different with central databases (IFRC, 2024a). The inputs of the citizens or the volunteers are also not usually included and there is no standardized channel, which adds more misinformation during such events, such as the 2024 locust inventions.
Gap: the optimal systems must have 95 percent data precision and multi-source integration (GIS with field uploads); the current situation is that dirty data results in incoherent actions, where 25 percent of relief products are wrongfully distributed because of information gaps (UNDRR, 2024a).
Economic Problems
Wastage of resources and high cost of operations associated with manual processes are economic inefficiencies. Repeated aid deliveries - due to a lack of visibility between agencies - squandered an estimated 15-20% of the 2023 relief budget (around 500 million ETB) of EDRMC when it was affected by multi-regional droughts as NGOs and regional offices procured and provided aid and supplies separately (EDRMC, 2023a). In the case of paper-based logistics tracking, there are extra costs of printing and transporting the paper documents which amount to 10-15 percent of the annual administrative cost.
Gap: A system that is optimized must cut in waste by 30-50 percent with automated distribution; the present system adds to recovery expenses by increasing the response time, which amounts to 2-5 billion ETB of unnecessary annual losses (World Bank Group, 2023b).
Control Problems
There is a lack of proper control and security measures as there is a lack of audit trail in manual methods and divided access controls in electronic applications. In 15% of shared reports, unauthorized data access or modifications happen because of the usage of email or physical documents without encryption as it is noted in a 2024 internal EDRMC security review. In the 2023 displacements due to the conflict, accountability was not maintained since 10% of the supplies were not accounted for.
Gap: The desired state is role-based access inclusive of audit logs and 99% traceability; existing vulnerabilities reveal sensitive data on beneficiaries, which may lead to non-conformity to Ethiopia Personal Data Protection Proclamation No. 1321/2024, and the risk of fraud increases by 20-25.
Efficiency Problems
The absence of automation and redundant manual processes threatens the operation efficiency since the staff must dedicate 60-70 percent of their time to data entry and verification instead of analysis (EDRMC, 2024). There is 12-24-hour delay in mobilizing resources because 10+ agencies engage in multiple in-person meetings and faxed forms which slow down coordination. As an example, three teams were involved in the assessment of the Oromia landslides in 2024 because there was no standard workflow.
Gap: The 40-60 percent efficiency improvements via automation are required because the present manual-intensive process slows down the total throughput of the process, which is now handling 50-70 incidents each month, where it should have been handling 150-plus (UNDRR, 2024a).
Service Problems
There are inaccessibility and timely provision of support to service delivery to the end-users such as the affected communities and first responders. The traditional SMS or radio-based early warnings are only 60 percent applicable to the vulnerable population in rural settings and there is no geo-targeted personalization, thus resulting in a low compliance rate 30 percent evacuation rate in 2023 floods (UNDRR, 2024a). Field agents complain of non-intuitive tools and up to 25 percent errors in forms submission.
Gap: The targeted level of service: 90 percent + coverage of alerts and user-friendly interfaces to use it offline; the current restrictions undermine the trust of the population and expose the stakeholders to more vulnerability since the current level of service satisfaction is at 45 percent (IFRC, 2024b).
To conclude, the PIECES framework reveals systemic flaws that amount to an estimated 30-40% inefficiency in EDRMC operations, which is translated into the long-term effects of disasters and increased socio-economic costs. These problems that can be measured, based on the concept of fragmentation and manual dependencies, define the needs of ISDREM directly since it will counter them with integration, automation and real-time features to promptly (by over 50% of the response efficacy) respond to them.

Non-functional requirements define the quality attributes, performance and constraints of a system, such as security, reliability and scalability. These ensure the system operates effectively under specified conditions and meets user expectations (Sommerville, 2016).
Non-Functional Requirements are crucial because they determine the overall system quality, usability, reliability, maintainability and scalability. For a critical application like ISDREM which operates under high-stress and real-time conditions fulfilling these requirements are mandatory. They address essential attributes like Performance, Security, Usability and Scalability. They are helpful in mitigating the system's current problems particularly concerning Control, Performance and Service Accessibility.

Business Rules Identification
Business rules are the policies and procedures, constraints and decision criteria governing the operations of ISDREM. They are based on Chapter 2 requirements, use case information (preconditions/postconditions), EDRMC stakeholder interviews (handwritten notes on ground data and alerts), National Disaster Risk Management Policy processes and such standards as DesInventar (mandatory DataCard fields: date, location, type, impacts) and ADPC roadmap (impact-based multi-hazard warnings). These guarantee the adherence to Sendai Framework, data integrity, data security and coordination between actors (Citizens, Registered Users, Administrators, Validators, Response Teams, Auditors, External Systems).
Business rules may be categorized as procedural rules (workflow and processes), validation rules (data accuracy and completeness), security rules (access control and authentication) and compliance rules (alignment to legal, ethical and international standards such as DesInventar classifications and strategies of ADPC roadmap) (EDRMC, 2023b; UNDRR, n.d.).
The following table contains the major business rules, identified, described, and sources (UC-IDs, where available) and traceability rationale in system design (e.g. class/sequence diagrams).
Rule ID
Category
Description
BR-001
Procedural
Incident reports must be submitted with mandatory geo-spatial data (latitude, longitude, timestamp), disaster type (e.g., drought, flood, fire, landslide, conflict), and impact details (e.g., deaths, injured, affected populations, houses destroyed/damaged, economic losses in USD/local currency). Reports without these fields are rejected.
BR-002
Validation
Alerts can only be issued after validation by at least one Validator or Admin role, with a severity level (Low/Medium/High) assigned based on predefined thresholds (e.g., affected population > 100 for High, probability of below-normal rainfall >50% for drought alerts). Alerts must include actionable instructions and be classified by hazard type.
BR-003
Security
User registration requires email verification, unique identifiers (e.g., email/username), and strong password policies (minimum 8 characters, including uppercase, lowercase, number, special character). Registered User roles are auto approved for Citizens, but Admin/Validator/Response Team roles require approval by an existing Admin.
BR-004
Procedural
Early warnings and alerts must be broadcasted through multiple channels (e.g., SMS, radio, social media, email, push notifications) to affected regions/woredas, with priority for high-severity incidents. Broadcasts include actionable instructions (e.g., "evacuate to safe areas") and must reference forecast probabilities (e.g., below-normal rainfall).
BR-005
Compliance
Post-disaster assessments (PDNA) must collect data on damages (e.g., loss of life, infrastructure, agriculture, crops in Hectares, cattle losses) and supporting evidence (e.g., photos, reports), stored for analysis and auditing. Assessments trigger resource allocation only if validated and must comply with Sendai Framework classifications.
BR-006
Validation
Resource allocation (e.g., aid, personnel) is restricted to verified incidents, with quantities limited by available stock (e.g., cannot allocate more than current inventory). Allocations must be logged in AuditLog with details on fulfillment status.
BR-007
Security
Access to sensitive data (e.g., incident details, analytics dashboards, audit logs) is role-based: Citizens view public alerts only; Validators verify reports; Admins manage users/alerts; Auditors review logs; Response Teams update status. External Systems (e.g., Payment Gateway) require API authentication.
BR-008
Compliance
All system actions (e.g., alert issuance, report submission) must comply with multilingual support (Amharic/English) and accessibility standards (e.g., high-contrast modes for alerts). Data must align with geographic divisions (e.g., regions like Oromia, Tigray).

A use case description provides a detailed narrative of how a user interacts with the system to achieve a specific goal, including the system's response to the user's actions. It helps in documenting functional requirements and ensuring that all possible scenarios are considered for proper system behavior (Cockburn, 2000).

It details how users perform certain actions such while ensuring a smooth and great experience. Chapter Four
Conclusion
The ISDREM was based on the crucial issues that had been highlighted in the Ethiopian disaster management environment, especially long delays in alert distribution, coordinating incidents, and allocating resources to the EDRMC and its collaborators.
With the help of a user-centered design and the use of modern technologies, i.e. real-time geospatial mapping (GIS integration), multi-channel alerts, role-based workflow, and the ability to see the resources and donations, ISDREM will deliver the right information to EDRMC staff, first responders and NGOs, as well as to the general population in a timely manner. The system allows people to quickly validate incidents, make informed decisions, optimally deploy resources, and have more transparency when it comes to disaster events.
The Scrum agile approach was applied to the project, which included stakeholder interviews through the iterative approach, document analysis, prototyping, and user testing to make the solution practical, scalable, and consistent with the national disaster risk management policies and standard operating procedures. According to evidence from global and local studies, integrated early warning and information systems can minimize the disaster damage by even 30 percent. ISDREM is at a strategic place to play a significant role in these results in Ethiopia by focusing on sealing the loopholes in existing processes, cutting down on response time, cutting down on redundancy of resources, and ultimately contributing towards saving lives and minimizing economic losses.
Although this project provides a practical and stakeholder-proven platform, it can be further advanced in the future with closer interaction with the already implemented national early warning systems and risk prediction using AI.
To sum up, ISDREM is an important milestone to a more resilient and coordinated disaster management system in Ethiopia, whereby the stakeholders will have the instruments they require to effectively respond to an ever more complex risk environment.
